Cool...atoms can actually stay together. What next?

Do they just hang around in empty space? Evidently not; you know from looking around you that atoms combine to make new stuff in innumerably different ways, from bananas to tabletops to computers.

So how do they do this? One way is through a chemical bond, where two or more atoms give or receive electrons to/from each other. On the right, you can see a neutral (no net electrical charge) sodium atom and a neutral chlorine atom. Let's watch them bond!

Chemical Bonding

Here it goes...

Firstly, let's get a better look at the atoms themselves, or rather, their electrons. The electrons in each atom aren't just hanging around in a random orbit; they're actually separated into regions of space known as energy shells. The closer to the atom the energy shell is, the less energy that electron has, and the more electrically bound it is to the nucleus.

You can see to the right how the electrons are laid out in their energy levels for both the neutral sodium and chlorine atom.

Of particular interest is the outermost shell, or valence shell. These electrons have the highest amount of energy and can't wait to separate from their atom.

Why is the valence shell interesting? It's because of a rule called the the octet rule that makes the valence shell "want" to have 8 electrons, even if it means the atom will no longer be electrically neutral. This sort of configuration is super-stable for atoms, so the octet rule will override electric repulsion and will be the driving force behind the bonding process we'll see next. Why does the octet rule exist?

Good question! The octet rule is a weird mathematical quirk that arises from a deeper law known as the Pauli Exclusion Principle, which basically says that two electrons cannot overlap too much. Having 8 electrons ensures a perfect distribution of electrons in space around the atom, so it's a super stable thing.

Now comes bonding time! The sodium atom just has one electron in its valence shell; it wants to get rid of that electron so that what's left is a full shell of 8 electrons. The chlorine atom has 7 electrons in its valence shell. It wants to have an extra electron to complete its shell!

So what happens is the optimal solution for both atoms: the sodium atom loses its valence electron and hands it over to the chlorine atom.

Now, the sodium atom is positively charged (since it was at first neutral, but lost an electron), and similarly, the chlorine atom is now negatively charged.

This difference in their electrical charge makes them attract each other, and now they're bonded!

Molecular Polarity

You just saw how chemical bonds can bring atoms together by making each atom electrically attracted to the other through an exchange of electrons. In the example of NaCl, the sodium atom ended up becoming positively charged and the chlorine atom became negatively charged. But does this mean molecules are neutral? It might seem so, since the total charge of the molecule balances out.

Here's a picture of a water molecule (H2O) that you're probably familiar with. In this molecule, each of the two hydrogen atoms shares one electron with the oxygen atom, which normally has 6 electrons in its valence shell.

Note the term share. The hydrogens don't give up their electrons, as sodium does to chlorine. This type of bond is called a covalent bond (you can think of it as a "cooperation bond"), while the sodium-chlorine bond was an ionic bond, because the sodium atom was ionized (stripped of electrons).

This is because the water molecule has electrical polarity. One side of it repels positive charges and the other repels negative charges. You can think of these sides as "poles", like the North and South poles of a magnet.
