The Coloumb Force

A Violent, but Balanced Force

You've just seen an example of how negative charges can repel negative charges, and how that repulsion depends on the distance between negative charges.

But have you ever heard the saying "opposites attract and like repels?" The concept actually has its origins in electricity. The counterpart of negative charges (the electron) are positive charges, which attract negative particles!

You can explore this idea in the same simulation as we had before. This time, press shift while you're clicking to place a positive charge (in this simulation, it's a special type of positive charge called a positron) instead of a negative one!

The positive charges move with pretty much the same speed, but in the opposite direction, right?

This is because everything about the particles we've got here is the same, except for their charge. The "oppositeness" of the charge basically flips the motion around! In fact, since this particular particle is exactly like an electron except for its charge, it's called a positron or sometimes even an antielectron. It's very different from a proton, which you may have heard is a component of the nucleus of atoms. The proton is a lot more massive than the positron, and is fundamentally a different type of matter.

You now know that like charges repel, but opposite charges attract. So I've got an interesting problem for you...

The Atom Conundrum

Protons attract electrons, and repel other protons. If you’re familiar with a basic model of the atom, you know that the nucleus is comprised of protons and non-electrically charged particles called neutrons. So why does the nucleus stay together?
