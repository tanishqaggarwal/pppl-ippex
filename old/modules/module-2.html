The puzzle

You've probably seen this model of the atom before. In the center is the nucleus, composed of positively charged particles, called protons, and particles with no charge, called neutrons. Electrons are spinning happily around the nucleus of the atom! But there's a problem here.

Why does the nucleus stay together at all? We know from the last unit that like charges repel; shouldn't the protons repel each other and make the nucleus blast apart?

Let's explore this.

The Strong Nuclear Force

What you just saw was the strong nuclear force in action. The strong nuclear force kicks in when the protons are REALLY close together and overpowers the repulsion of the Coloumb force.

Here's a good analogy for this: you can think of the protons as two balls with a hook on them, separated by a spring. Usually, the spring (the electric force) pushes the protons back. But if the protons push together with enough energy, their hooks lock together and keep them together despite the repulsion of the spring.

What's more...E=mc2

Think of what happens when things collide. You hear a bang, right? In other words, energy is released in an often violent, explosive way.
With the strong nuclear force, things get even more intense. When the protons fuse, they both lose a little bit of mass. Let's explore what happens. (Play animation)

There are a couple of weird principles that come into play. One is Einstein's famous equation, E = mc2, which basically says that mass is the same thing as energy.

But the big thing is this: the c in the equation is the speed of light, a HUGE number (11 BILLION miles per hour!). You can see that even the tiniest bits of matter, when multiplied by c SQUARED, contain a ton of energy.

The second idea is the law of conservation of energy. This is important, because the proton mass can't just "disappear"--what happens is that it's converted into energy that's dissipated throughout the system.
